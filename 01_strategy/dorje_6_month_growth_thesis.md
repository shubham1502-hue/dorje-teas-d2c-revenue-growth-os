# Dorje Teas — 6-Month D2C Revenue Growth Thesis

> Strategic operating sequence for a Founder's Office / Revenue Ops operator.
> Based on public information and category analysis. No internal Dorje data used.
> All figures are synthetic and illustrative.

---

## Starting Point: What the Operating System Must Hold Together

Dorje is not a standard D2C brand where more spend reliably produces more revenue. It operates in a category with several compounding constraints:

- **Trust gap before first purchase** — premium, estate-grown, unfamiliar to most buyers. Education is required, not optional.
- **Natural product seasonality** — First Flush and Second Flush are harvest-timed windows. Missing them is not recoverable in-year.
- **Multiple buyer types** — Daily Premium Tea Drinker, Darjeeling Loyalist, Gift Buyer, Health/Wellness Consumer, and Climate/Story-Led Buyer. These segments have different acquisition channels, conversion triggers, and retention behavior.
- **Subscription potential** — Tea Club is a predictable-revenue lever, but subscription pushed too early creates churn. It must be earned, not forced.
- **Margin sensitivity** — premium packaging, direct-from-Darjeeling logistics, and small average order sizes make contribution margin per order thin before scale.

The 6-month operating thesis must hold acquisition, conversion, retention, gifting, and margin together — not optimize each in isolation.

---

## The Core Tension

> Most D2C brands optimize for ROAS and new customer count.
> Dorje must optimize for **contribution-margin-positive repeat revenue**.

That shift changes everything:

| Optimizing for ROAS | Optimizing for CM-positive repeat revenue |
|---|---|
| Scale spend when ROAS is positive | Scale spend when CM is positive after all costs |
| First purchase is success | First purchase is the start of the measurement |
| CAC is the acquisition metric | LTV:CAC is the acquisition metric |
| New customers = growth | Returning customers = sustainable growth |
| Channel efficiency = clicks and conversions | Channel efficiency = cohort behavior over 90 days |

## 6-Month Operating Sequence

### Phase 1: Revenue Visibility (Months 1-2)

**Objective:** Know where revenue is actually coming from before adding pressure.

**What gets built:**
- Shopify order export → channel, SKU, new vs. returning, discount flag, city
- Google Ads and Meta campaign export → spend, impressions, CTR, CPC, CVR, orders
- Email and WhatsApp platform data → subscriber count, open rate, click rate by segment
- Product COGS, packaging, shipping, and gateway fee inputs from finance/ops
- Weekly revenue dashboard (first draft, manually maintained if needed)

**What gets measured for the first time:**
- CAC by channel (not just ROAS)
- Contribution margin by channel and by product type
- Repeat purchase rate (what % of Month 1 buyers return in Month 2?)
- Time to second purchase
- Subscription attach rate by SKU
- AOV by product category
- Cart abandonment rate and add-to-cart-to-checkout gap

**Decision at end of Phase 1:**
> Where are the top 3 revenue leaks? What is the priority order for Phase 2?

**What does not happen in Phase 1:**
- Scaling paid acquisition before baseline is established
- Launching new campaigns without tracking contribution margin
- Calling any channel efficient based on ROAS alone

---

### Phase 2: First-Purchase Conversion (Months 2-3)

**Objective:** Reduce the cost of a first purchase before increasing acquisition spend.

**What gets tested:**
- Product page asset sequencing: does benefit → price-per-cup → origin proof → reviews → brewing guide → subscription prompt outperform the current layout?
- Landing page message-match: does a campaign for "First Flush Darjeeling" land on a First Flush-specific page or a generic shop page?
- Price-per-cup framing: does adding "approximately ₹X per cup" near the price increase CVR?
- First-time buyer bundle: does offering a curated sampler (First Flush + Chai + one more) as an entry offer increase first-purchase CVR and AOV simultaneously?
- Checkout friction audit: where does the drop from add-to-cart to purchase happen, and what is causing it?
- Free-shipping threshold: is the cart threshold set at the right AOV level to add revenue without reducing completion rate?

**Metrics being moved:**
- Add-to-cart rate (current → target)
- Checkout rate (current → target)
- First-purchase CVR (current → target)
- First-purchase AOV (current → target)
- Contribution margin on first order

**Decision at end of Phase 2:**
> Which conversion lever moved the most revenue per rupee of effort? What gets scaled in Phase 3?

---

### Phase 3: Repeat Purchase and Retention (Months 3-4)

**Objective:** Turn first-time buyers into profitable returning customers before spending more to acquire new ones.

**What gets built:**
- Day 7 post-purchase: brewing education email or WhatsApp — how to brew the specific tea they bought, tasting notes, what to expect
- Day 21: replenishment nudge — "your first 100g pack is likely running low" with a direct re-order link
- Day 30: cross-sell — "you bought First Flush, you might love Second Flush" or "ready for something everyday? Try the Chai"
- Day 45: subscription prompt — "you've reordered once. Would you like us to send it automatically?" — only triggered after demonstrated repeat behavior
- Gift Buyer track (separate): Gift Buyer receives a different Day 7 message — "if you loved what you gifted, here is the same for yourself"
- Winback trigger: anyone who has not engaged after Day 60 enters a winback segment

**Metrics being moved:**
- Repeat purchase rate (Month 1 cohort returning in Month 3)
- Time to second purchase
- Subscription attach rate after second purchase
- Email and WhatsApp click-to-purchase rate
- Winback reactivation rate

**Decision at end of Phase 3:**
> What is the repeat purchase rate for the cohort acquired in Phase 1? Is the retention loop working or are we still paying full CAC for every order?

---

### Phase 4: Gifting and Seasonal Demand (Months 4-5)

**Objective:** Build separate operating tracks for high-intent seasonal windows.

**Why a separate track:**
Gifting buyers are not the same as daily tea buyers. They have higher AOV, lower repeat rate on their own behalf, higher sensitivity to packaging and presentation, and a different post-purchase lifecycle. Treating them with the same flows as daily buyers is a retention failure.

**What gets built:**

*Gifting track:*
- Gift box dedicated landing page: estate story, curated contents, premium packaging proof, delivery promise
- Corporate gifting inquiry path: form or WhatsApp entry point for bulk orders
- Post-Gift Buyer follow-up: "you sent Dorje as a gift — here is the same for yourself" — self-purchase conversion attempt
- Gift receiver path (if email capture is possible via gifting flow): introduce the receiver to Dorje and offer a first-self-purchase discount

*First Flush track (if still in season):*
- First Flush Darjeeling dedicated landing page
- Harvest story content: what makes this year's flush different, when it was picked, tasting notes
- Limited batch framing: communicates genuine harvest scarcity without manufactured urgency

**Metrics being moved:**
- Gift box CVR and AOV
- Corporate gifting inquiry volume and conversion
- Gift Buyer to self-purchaser conversion rate
- First Flush page CVR and revenue per session
- Gifting contribution margin (premium AOV must cover premium packaging)

**Decision at end of Phase 4:**
> What is the gifting contribution margin? Do Gift Buyer customers self-purchase at a rate that justifies the acquisition cost?

---

### Phase 5: Margin-Aware Scale (Months 5-6)

**Objective:** Scale only what has proven contribution-margin-positive. Use 5 months of cohort data to make channel and offer decisions.

**What the decision framework looks like:**

Illustrative thresholds only; replace with real cohort benchmarks once internal data is available.

| Signal | Action |
|---|---|
| Channel has positive CM, improving repeat rate, and LTV:CAC above 2x | Scale spend |
| Channel has positive ROAS but negative CM after shipping and packaging | Fix margin before scaling |
| Channel has good CVR but high churn from subscription cohort | Fix the post-subscription experience before scaling |
| Channel has high CAC but excellent repeat rate and subscription attach | Keep at current spend, build retention depth |
| Channel consistently produces one-time buyers with no repeat signal | Pause or redirect spend |
| Gifting campaign has high AOV but zero self-purchase conversion | Reframe post-Gift Buyer follow-up before next gifting window |

**What gets reviewed monthly in Phase 5:**
- Cohort revenue by acquisition month (Month 1, 2, 3, 4)
- LTV:CAC by channel
- Subscription active count, renewal rate, monthly churn
- Contribution margin by product and channel
- Experiment learnings: what was scaled, fixed, or killed

**Decision at end of Phase 5 (also the 6-month review):**
> What does the next 6 months look like? Which channels scale with confidence? What is the contribution-margin-positive revenue run rate?

## What This Thesis Is Not Claiming

This thesis is a **public-information and category-logic based operating hypothesis**.

It does not:
- Use internal Dorje revenue, order, or campaign data
- Claim that Dorje has specific CAC, ROAS, repeat rate, or margin problems
- Guarantee that these phases will produce specific revenue outcomes
- Replace the need for real data-driven iteration once internal access is available

It is designed to show how an operator would **structure the first 6 months** of a Founder's Office revenue engagement — with clear objectives, measurable outputs, and decision points at each phase.

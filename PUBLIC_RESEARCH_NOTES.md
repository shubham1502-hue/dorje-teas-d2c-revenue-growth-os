# Public Research Notes  -  Dorje Teas

> Everything in this file is based on publicly available information.
> No internal data, internal conversations, or proprietary research was used.
> Observations are used to inform the operating system design, not to audit Dorje's actual performance.

---

## 1. Brand Positioning

Dorje Teas positions itself around estate provenance, not blend or price. The core claim is whole-leaf Darjeeling tea plucked, packed, and dispatched from Selim Hill Tea Estate  -  a certified organic heritage garden in Darjeeling.

This is a meaningful differentiation in the Indian premium tea market, where most brands sell blended teas of unclear origin. Dorje's positioning creates a direct line between the consumer's cup and a specific, named, traceable place.

Key positioning signals observed publicly:
- "Selim Hill Tea Garden" used as origin marker, not just a brand name
- "Whole leaf" and "single estate" language used consistently
- Organic certification surfaced in brand communication
- Freshness and harvest timing (First Flush, Second Flush) used as product framing
- Founder/estate connection implied in the brand narrative

**D2C implication:** Premium positioning requires higher trust investment before first purchase. Landing pages, product pages, and email flows cannot skip the "why should I trust this" question. Trust-building content is not optional  -  it is conversion infrastructure.

---

## 2. Alt Carbon Connection

Dorje Teas operates within the Alt Carbon ecosystem. Alt Carbon's public thesis is centered on Darjeeling revival: improving soil health, working with tea estate owners and farmers, measuring carbon removal, and connecting the economic health of Darjeeling's tea community with land and ecological revival.

This gives Dorje a brand narrative layer that most D2C tea brands cannot access:
- A specific named estate (Selim Hill)
- A soil and land revival story
- A climate and carbon connection
- A community and farmer connection
- A long-term Darjeeling revival arc

**D2C implication:** This narrative is an underutilized acquisition and retention lever. Buyers in the premium tea category increasingly overlap with climate-aware, origin-conscious consumers. Story-led content connected to Selim Hill, carbon, and revival could meaningfully improve consideration-stage conversion and post-purchase emotional engagement (which drives repeat purchase and referrals).

## 3. Product Catalog Observations

Based on Dorje's public product catalog, the following product types are visible:

| Category | Observed Products | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| First Flush | First Flush Darjeeling | Seasonal, highest-intent product |
| Second Flush | Second Flush Darjeeling | Muscatel character, premium positioning |
| Green Tea | Darjeeling Green Tea | Expanding beyond classic black tea |
| Chai | Original Darjeeling Chai blend | Mass-appeal, higher replenishment frequency |
| Cold Brew | Cold Brew Darjeeling | Modern format, younger consumer angle |
| Subscription | Tea Club | Recurring model, likely subscription box |

**Observations:**
- Product ladder moves from premium/seasonal (First Flush) to accessible/daily (Chai, Cold Brew)
- This creates a natural acquisition-to-retention product architecture if deployed intentionally
- First Flush and Second Flush are the highest-differentiation products; Chai and Cold Brew are the highest-frequency replenishment products
- Tea Club/subscription is the revenue predictability lever

**D2C implication:** Not all products should be acquired through the same channel or with the same landing page. First Flush deserves a harvest-specific page. Chai deserves a daily ritual framing. Cold Brew deserves a modern, urban, health-adjacent angle. Bundling these on a single generic product listing dilutes all three.

## 4. Pricing Observations

Pricing was not extracted in detail, but the general tier observed is consistent with premium D2C Indian tea brands: above mass-market supermarket pricing, below luxury/artisanal gift-only pricing.

This positions Dorje in the "affordable premium" zone  -  aspirational enough to require trust, accessible enough to repeat purchase without guilt.

**D2C implication:** Price-per-cup framing could unlock conversion for hesitant first-time buyers. A 100g First Flush pack at a premium price point looks expensive at face value but reasonable when framed as 40–50 cups of exceptional Darjeeling tea at ₹X per cup.

---

## 5. Subscription and Tea Club Observations

Tea Club appears to be Dorje's subscription or community-facing offer. The public presence suggests:
- Recurring tea delivery model
- Community or curation element to the membership

**D2C implication:** Tea Club is the highest-LTV product in the portfolio if managed well. Subscription economics depend on:
- Attach rate (what % of buyers convert to subscription)
- Trial-to-active rate (what % of first-subscription-order buyers renew)
- Renewal rate (month-to-month or seasonal)
- Churn reason diagnosis (product fatigue, delivery issues, price sensitivity, or just forgot)

Subscription pushed too early (before trust and repeat habit are built) creates high early churn and a misleading active subscriber metric.

## 6. D2C Funnel Observations

Based on public website structure and product page design patterns:

- Direct website D2C appears to be a primary sales channel
- Marketplace presence may exist (Amazon, specialty retailers) but owned D2C is the focus for margin and data control
- Product pages carry origin and product storytelling, suggesting the brand understands that premium tea needs education, not just a buy button
- No aggressive discount popup or coupon wall was observed, which is consistent with premium positioning intent. However, the site does show promotional pricing on gift boxes, pyramid teabags, subscriptions, and at least one Buy 1 Get 1 banner on a Second Flush product. The more accurate read: Dorje is not popup-discount-led, but visible promotional mechanics exist. Discount rate and contribution margin impact should be tracked carefully.

**D2C implication:** The absence of aggressive discounting is correct for long-term brand health but means conversion rate may be lower than category average. This is acceptable only if the post-purchase experience builds the repeat loop. If first-time buyers are not coming back, the brand is paying full CAC for every purchase indefinitely.

## 7. Existing Conversion Assets on the Website

Dorje already has several public-facing conversion assets visible on the site:

- Product-level review counts
- "As seen on" trust signals
- "Brewing Tea for over 1,00,000 customers" social proof
- Brewing guide sections on product pages
- Product FAQs explaining origin, freshness, people, and organic status
- Marketplace and "Available On" trust blocks
- Product-level purchase options including one-time and subscription choices on relevant SKUs
- Cart-level free shipping threshold messaging

**D2C implication:** Dorje does not need to invent trust infrastructure from zero. The opportunity is to measure whether these assets are placed at the right point in the funnel and whether they improve add-to-cart rate, checkout conversion, and first-purchase CVR.

**Operator hypothesis:** Product pages should be tested around asset sequencing:
1. Product benefit and taste profile
2. Price-per-cup framing
3. Origin and estate proof (Selim Hill, 1871, organic, Rainforest certification)
4. Review and social proof
5. Brewing guide
6. Subscription or replenishment prompt

## 8. Subscription Architecture Observation

Dorje's public site shows subscription as a visible commercial line, not just a brand or community idea. The Tea Club page includes plan options with size options (100g, 250g) and delivery frequency options (monthly, quarterly). Some product pages also expose subscription purchase options directly at the SKU level.

**D2C implication:** Subscription should be tracked at two levels:

| Subscription Layer | What to Track |
|---|---|
| Tea Club plan-level subscription | Page CVR, plan selection, renewal rate, churn, subscriber LTV |
| Product-level subscription | Subscription attach rate by SKU, first renewal, product-level churn, subscription contribution margin |

**Operator hypothesis:** Subscription should not be pushed uniformly to all first-time buyers. Dorje should test subscription prompts after product education, second purchase, high engagement, or replenishment behavior  -  not on the first visit.

## 9. Pricing, Discounting, and Margin Observation

Dorje's public site shows premium pricing but also visible promotional mechanics across some categories: discounted gift boxes, discounted pyramid teabags, subscription offer pricing, and at least one promotional banner on a core product.

**D2C implication:** The operating system should not assume the brand is fully non-discount-led. The better question is whether discounts are being used as controlled conversion levers or are becoming the primary reason to purchase.

**Metrics to track:**
- Discount rate by channel
- Discount rate by SKU
- Discount rate by gift bundle
- Discounted revenue vs. full-price revenue
- Contribution margin after discount
- Repeat purchase rate of discounted buyers vs. full-price buyers
- Subscription churn from discounted subscribers

**Operator hypothesis:** Discounts can support trial, bundles, and seasonal campaigns. They should not become the core premium value proposition.

---

## 10. Logistics and Freshness Promise

Dorje's product FAQs publicly communicate a direct-from-Darjeeling promise: tea is roasted again before leaving the factory, dispatched directly to customers with no middlemen when bought from the website, and reaches customers within a stated window after dispatch from Darjeeling.

**D2C implication:** Logistics is not only an ops function  -  it is part of the conversion promise.

The founder dashboard should track:
- Delivery timeline adherence vs. stated promise
- Shipping cost per order
- Failed delivery or delay rate
- Replacement and refund rate
- Region-wise logistics cost
- Customer complaints linked to delivery or freshness

**Operator hypothesis:** If Dorje is using freshness and direct-from-estate fulfillment as a trust lever, delivery reliability and freshness perception should be reviewed alongside conversion and repeat purchase metrics  -  not separately.

Based on the public catalog, Dorje's product range should be treated as multiple commercial motions operating in parallel, not one generic tea shop.

| Product Type | Likely Commercial Role |
|---|---|
| First Flush / Second Flush | Premium acquisition, seasonal intent, Darjeeling Loyalist segment |
| Darjeeling Green Tea / Cold Brew Darjeeling | Health and routine-led acquisition |
| Original Darjeeling Chai | Daily consumption and replenishment anchor |
| Pyramid Teabags | Convenience, trial, office use, gifting add-on |
| Cold Brew | Younger consumer discovery, modern Darjeeling format |
| Selim Hill Gift Box Classic and Selim Hill Gift Box Premium | Seasonal and high-AOV gifting |
| Tea Club / Subscriptions | LTV ceiling, retention, predictable revenue |

**D2C implication:** Each product type should have its own landing page logic, campaign angle, margin view, and post-purchase follow-up. A single blended revenue report would hide where revenue quality is actually coming from.

## 12. Cart and AOV Observation

Dorje's cart shows free-shipping threshold messaging, visible when a cart is below the free-shipping minimum. This is a live AOV and conversion lever.

**D2C implication:** The cart should be reviewed as part of the revenue funnel, not just checkout UX.

**Metrics to track:**
- Cart-to-checkout rate
- AOV before and after free-shipping threshold
- Add-on or upsell attach rate inside cart
- Shipping threshold completion rate
- Contribution margin after threshold-driven upsells

**Operator hypothesis:** Free-shipping threshold messaging can improve AOV, but only if the incremental product added has healthy margin and does not reduce checkout completion rate by adding decision friction.

## 13. Category and Competitive Landscape (Public Observation)

The premium Indian D2C tea market includes several operators:

| Brand Type | Differentiation Angle | Dorje's Position vs. This |
|---|---|---|
| Heritage estate brands | Origin, authenticity | Dorje's core strength  -  Selim Hill is specific and traceable |
| Wellness/functional tea brands | Health claims, ingredients | Less relevant for Dorje's current positioning |
| Gifting-first tea brands | Premium packaging, brand name | Dorje has gifting potential but needs bundle/gifting architecture |
| Marketplace tea brands | Price, volume, discovery | Dorje should not compete here  -  margin and positioning dilution risk |
| Specialty/artisanal importers | Rare origins, collector appeal | Adjacent; First Flush seasonality creates overlap |

**D2C implication:** Dorje's defensible position is estate-origin specificity + sustainability narrative. Neither of these can be copied quickly by commodity brands. The revenue OS should build around defending and monetizing this moat, not racing into category-generic channels.

## 14. Seasonal and Campaign Calendar (Inferred from Category)

Darjeeling tea has natural seasonal moments that should anchor the D2C campaign calendar:

| Season | Event | D2C Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| March–April | First Flush harvest | Highest-intent seasonal acquisition window |
| May–June | Second Flush harvest | Follow-up seasonal campaign |
| October–November | Diwali / festive season | Premium gifting window |
| November–December | Winter / holiday gifting | Corporate gifting, bulk gift box window |
| January | New Year / wellness | Health-positioning angle for Green Tea, Cold Brew |
| Year-round | Daily chai consumption | Replenishment-led retention |

**D2C implication:** Treating every month the same is a waste of seasonal intent. The campaign budget, creative, and landing page should shift by season. First Flush is a window that closes  -  it cannot be made up later in the year.

---

## Summary: What Public Research Tells Us About the D2C Operating System

1. **Instrument what already exists**  -  Dorje has trust assets, conversion mechanics, subscription products, and a defined product ladder. The operator opportunity is to measure, sequence, and optimize them  -  not invent strategy from scratch.
2. **Trust before conversion**  -  premium positioning requires education infrastructure at the right funnel stage, not just a buy button
3. **Product ladder is real**  -  acquisition products (First Flush, bundles), replenishment products (Chai, subscriptions), and gifting products (gift boxes) each need separate operating logic
4. **Seasonality is structural**  -  the campaign calendar must account for harvest windows and gifting seasons as distinct commercial tracks
5. **Story is a growth lever**  -  Selim Hill, Alt Carbon, and Darjeeling revival are underutilized conversion and retention assets already grounded in the product FAQ
6. **Subscription is the LTV ceiling**  -  Tea Club and SKU-level subscriptions done well are the highest-margin, most predictable revenue lines
7. **Discounting needs tracking**  -  visible promotional mechanics mean discount rate and contribution margin must be monitored, not assumed away
8. **Logistics is a brand promise**  -  the freshness and direct-from-Darjeeling claim must be backed by delivery performance tracking alongside conversion metrics

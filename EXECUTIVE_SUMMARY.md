# Executive Summary — Dorje Teas D2C Revenue Growth OS

> Operator memo. Written as if addressed to Dorje's founding team.
> All figures are synthetic. No internal data was used.

---

## The Growth Problem

Dorje Teas is not competing on price. It is competing on origin, trust, and taste. That means the revenue system cannot be optimized around the same levers as a commodity D2C brand.

The real growth problem is not "how do we get more traffic." It is:

> How do we convert premium-positioned traffic into first-time buyers, turn first-time buyers into repeat customers, move repeat customers toward subscription, and do all of this while keeping contribution margin positive?

Most D2C brands optimize for ROAS and GMV. Dorje needs to optimize for **contribution-margin-positive repeat D2C revenue**.

---

## 6-Month Revenue Thesis

**Phase 1: Revenue Visibility (Months 1-2)**
Install revenue visibility before scaling spend. Map the funnel, baseline CAC/ROAS/CTR/CVR, AOV, repeat purchase rate, subscription behavior, gifting revenue, and contribution margin. The goal is to identify the two or three biggest revenue leaks before adding more acquisition pressure.

**Phase 2: First-Purchase Conversion (Months 2-3)**
Fix the conversion layer. Test product page clarity, landing page-message match, first-time buyer bundles, price-per-cup framing, trust assets, and checkout friction. The goal is to reduce the cost of first purchase before increasing paid acquisition.

**Phase 3: Repeat Purchase and Retention (Months 3-4)**
Build the repeat purchase loop. Use brewing education, product-specific replenishment reminders, cross-sell flows, winback flows, and subscription prompts timed to actual consumption behavior. The goal is to turn first-time buyers into profitable returning customers.

**Phase 4: Gifting and Seasonal Demand (Months 4-5)**
Build separate operating tracks for seasonal harvest demand and gifting. First Flush, Second Flush, festive gifting, corporate gifting, and premium bundles should have their own landing pages, margin assumptions, campaign windows, and repeat-purchase follow-up logic.

**Phase 5: Margin-Aware Scale (Months 5-6)**
Scale only what has proven contribution-margin-positive. Use CAC, ROAS, LTV:CAC, repeat purchase rate, cohort behavior, and contribution margin to decide what to scale, fix, pause, or kill. Weekly ROAS should inform decisions, but it should not be the final decision metric.

## North Star Metric

> **contribution-margin-positive repeat D2C revenue**

Not GMV. Not ROAS alone. Not new customer count alone.

The metric that matters: **are we acquiring customers who come back profitably?**

---

## Top 3 Growth Bets (Months 1–6)

**1. Retention loop before more paid acquisition**
Most premium tea brands lose first-time buyers before they repurchase. A 7-day brewing education sequence, 21-day replenishment nudge, and 45-day subscription prompt carries low marginal cost and compounds with every new customer acquired.

**2. First Flush seasonal conversion window**
First Flush is Dorje's highest-signal seasonal moment. A dedicated landing page with estate origin story, tasting notes, price-per-cup framing, and a curated bundle offer can meaningfully lift first-purchase CVR during this high-intent window.

**3. Contribution margin visibility before scaling spend**
Scaling paid acquisition without knowing contribution margin per channel leads to ROAS-positive but margin-negative outcomes. Install a simple contribution margin view by channel before increasing spend.

## Key Risks

| Risk | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Paid acquisition scales but CM collapses | Discounting, shipping, or COGS absorbs margin at scale |
| Premium positioning diluted by discount pressure | Destroys repeat purchase economics and brand perception |
| Subscription push before product trust is built | Increases early churn, weakens the metric permanently |
| Seasonal spikes create fulfillment/inventory pressure | Especially around First Flush and festive gifting |
| Gift Buyer segment treated like Daily Premium Tea Drinker segment | Wrong lifecycle messaging, low repeat conversion |
| Marketplace presence confuses owned D2C measurement | Attribution becomes unreliable |

## What the Founder Should Inspect Every Monday

1. Did revenue grow week-over-week? What drove it — new customers, returning customers, or AOV?
2. Which campaign had the best contribution-margin-positive performance?
3. Where is the funnel leaking — traffic, product page, cart, or checkout?
4. How many customers bought for the second time this week?
5. Is any experiment ready for a scale, fix, or kill decision?
